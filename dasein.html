Design for Dasein by Wendt

Background

I had the privilege of editing Design for Dasein: Understanding the Design of Experiences, a publication authored by my mentor and friend, Thomas Wendt—a UX strategist who taught me what it means to be a "UX thinker" in my first-ever design internship.

A philosopher at heart, Thomas writes about the interrelation between phenomenology (the study of human experience), experience design (the act of intentionally crafting the effects of a user’s interaction with a system), and design thinking (a style of thinking that fits solutions into a problem context based on insights gained through empathy).

My Role

I focused on stylistic editing and accessibility, as Thomas wanted to reach designers and non-designers alike. The challenge in the original manuscript was in its academic texbook-like nature. My role was to edit the content to be accessible to designers from a range of backgrounds—from HCI psychology to graphic design—without oversimplifying the author's voice and message.

About the Book

"Dasein" literally translates from German to "being there", but is a term used to refer to "human existence" by Heidegger, the existential philosopher.

Taking a Heideggerian approach, Design for Dasein is a fascinating take on User Experience as more than a field of study—it is a philosophy rooted in being (human) and using technology as a means to understand the world around us. Though posing more questions than answering, Thomas approaches the topic socratically to help readers challenge en vogue methodologies; revisit and possibly evolve our long-held conceptions of the design process; think etymologically about words like "empathy" that we tend to thrown around; and finally, pop the bubble of "best practices" and instead see evolving problem spaces as a starting point to designing solutions. Clearly written and researched, this is an eye-opening read that enlightens but does not indoctrinate. It gives designer-readers the philosophical foundation for the practice we're to ensue.
